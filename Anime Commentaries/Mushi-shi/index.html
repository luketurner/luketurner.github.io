Mushi-shi

Rating: ★★★★☆

Mushi-shi is a rare beast: a truly episodic anime series. Each episode is completely self-contained, and only Ginko, the protagonist, appears in more than one (he appears in all of them).

Being truly episodic lends Mushi-shi an air of atemporality. Although the order of episodes is clearly not arbitrary [1], it is equally clear that it could be. Each episode contains a self-contained story, and they don’t reference each other at all. There is no shared context. Even the description of the mushi — the only extranormal aspect of the show — as life-forms that exist in a separate mode from the usual flora and fauna, is repeated almost every episode.

Nevertheless, Mushi-shi manages a cohesive (and quite compelling) storyline. Ginko is a traveling "mushi master", who thanks to some strange encounters with mushi as a youth, now attracts them. If he stays in one place, the mushi bring trouble to people around him. So he wanders from village to village, using his expertise to solve mushi-related problems, and conducting research on the mushi at the same time. Counter-intuitively, an episodic storyline induces a strong sensation of permanence. Although the particular circumstances of Ginko’s life vary from day to day, his general circumstances never change — such a change would introduce context that would disrupt the atemporal episodicity. In this way, the show lacks almost everything that traditionally makes a story. It doesn’t bother with suspense or closure, or progression, or climax, except in bite-size segments. As a result, it is extremely calming.

The powerful peace of Mushi-shi lies not only in the show’s episodicity but also in the strength of the protagonist and the expanse of the setting. Ginko’s strength is his detachment and his rare and valuable knowledge. The setting’s expansiveness is a vast natural terrain pockmarked with small villages. It’s the kind of world where consequences are mitigated, and Ginko is the kind of protagonist who can do no wrong: he is protected by some of the strongest, least explained, and yet least grating "plot armor" I’ve ever seen. The combination of these factors with the show’s anti-story construction produces an experience that is almost spiritual in its preternatural, Zen-like routine. The result is, on inspection, completely debased from our uncertain reality, and yet exemplifies the equanimity and natural transience of the enlightened. Like much of the best fiction, Mushi-shi does not reflect reality, but uses storytelling to evoke emotion. However, instead of hope, love, despair or regret, that emotion is simple tranquility.

[1]How do we know it’s not arbitrary? I think the main clue is that each season ends with an episode that provides backstory about the main character. Perhaps this backstory is intended as a type of closure.
